\begin{abstract}
Cell sleep can reduce radio access network energy use, but load shifts can turn
apparently safe sleep decisions into quality-of-service violations.  This paper
presents UNO-UMB, an ns-3 prototype for uncertainty-aware cell sleep control.
UNO-UMB combines a lightweight digital-twin risk gate, a latent-demand wakeup
rule for unannounced shifts, and a forecast-risk margin that can be selectively
inflated for tight close-offload decisions.  In an expanded LTE evaluation over
five seeds, three runs, and three inter-site spacings, the adaptive wakeup
recovers all 44/44 feasible center-burst rows where static risk gating is safe
on 40/44.  On a right-edge transition surface with a 25\% underpredicted
forecast, a base margin improves static control from 33/40 to 37/40 safe
feasible rows.  A stronger global margin reaches 39/40 safety but lowers mean
safe energy saving to 9.927\%, while a calibrated selective margin reaches the
same 39/40 safety with 13.754\% mean safe energy saving.  These results
suggest that spatially conditioned uncertainty control can preserve energy
savings while reducing transition risk.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Radio access networks are increasingly energy constrained: base stations account
for a large share of cellular network energy demand, and deployments are often
dimensioned for busy periods even though traffic varies sharply over time
\cite{auer2011energy,hasan2011green}.  Cell sleep is a direct way to reduce
active energy, yet a sleep decision changes the load and radio geometry seen by
neighboring cells.  The central difficulty is therefore not only to find idle
cells, but to decide which apparently idle cells can sleep without creating a
quality-of-service violation after traffic shifts.

UNO-UMB studies this problem in ns-3.  The current prototype focuses on a
simple but paper-relevant question: can a lightweight uncertainty-aware
controller recover energy savings while avoiding controller-induced failures in
workloads that remain feasible when all cells stay active?

The contributions are:

\begin{itemize}
\item A digital-twin cell sleep controller that evaluates candidate sleep
decisions against per-UE offered load and predicted active-cell utilization.
\item A latent-demand wakeup mechanism that reacts to unannounced load shifts
by reactivating sleeping preferred cells when doing so would relieve active
cell stress.
\item A forecast-risk margin with selective overlap inflation, which applies a
stronger bound only when a tight utilization decision also uses close offload
geometry.
\item A feasible-row evaluation method that separates controller-induced
violations from offered-load overload by comparing every controller row against
the matching all-on reference row.
\end{itemize}

\section{Controller Design}

UNO-UMB operates as a periodic cell sleep controller with event-triggered
reevaluation when demand changes.  For each candidate cell, the controller
estimates whether sleeping the cell would overload the remaining active cells.
The baseline twin policy keeps a cell active when the estimated risk exceeds a
configured guard.

The adaptive controller adds a latent-demand wakeup gate.  When a sleeping cell
is the preferred serving cell for enough offered demand, and waking it would
reduce peak active-cell utilization by a configured relief threshold, the
controller reactivates that cell.  This mechanism targets unannounced load
shifts where a previously safe sleep decision becomes unsafe before the next
steady-state placement.

For anticipated traffic shifts, UNO-UMB supports a lead-time forecast gate.  A
forecast can carry both a magnitude error and an uncertainty margin.  The
selective overlap variant uses a base uncertainty margin for ordinary forecast
decisions, then inflates the margin only when the candidate decision has small
utilization slack and nearby offload geometry.  This keeps the base margin on
placements where it is already sufficient and avoids charging a stronger global
margin to every forecasted decision.

\section{Evaluation Setup}

The prototype is implemented as an ns-3 contribution module and evaluated using
an LTE cell sleep example, following the use of ns-3 LTE models as an open
simulation substrate for radio-resource and self-optimization studies
\cite{baldo2011lte}.  Unless otherwise noted, the main rows use 16 UEs, a
1.0 Mb/s per-UE base offered load, a 12 s simulation, and a burst window from
3 s to 10 s over five seeds, three runs, and three inter-site spacings.  The
expanded controller table uses ideal LTE RRC signaling to avoid treating
stressed over-the-air control-plane scheduler assertions as cell-sleep
controller outcomes.

The evaluation reports three classes of outcome.  First, the all-on reference
identifies whether the offered-load scenario is feasible without cell sleep.
Second, controller rows are counted only when the matching all-on row is
feasible.  Third, safe energy saving is measured only for controller rows that
avoid induced violations.

The forecast-margin result is the strongest current evidence for the paper.  A
base margin improves right-edge safety by four feasible rows relative to
static no-forecast control.  The calibrated selective margin improves safety by
two more rows, matching the global 0.35 margin at 39/40 safe feasible rows, but
preserves 13.754\% mean safe energy saving instead of the global margin's
9.927\%.  The residual unsafe row differs between the global and selective
settings, showing that transition safety is not monotone in conservatism.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}

The present results are intentionally framed as feasible-row results, not as a
claim that every offered-load scenario can be made safe by control.  Some rows
are overloaded even when all cells remain active, and those rows are excluded
from controller safety rates.

The next validation step is a broader sweep over UE rates, UE counts, and
additional traffic geometries.  The prototype should also be compared against
stronger baselines, including controllers with calibrated static margins and
controllers that use forecast lead time without spatially selective inflation.

\section{Related Work}

RAN energy-saving work has long identified base-station sleep, adaptive
coverage, and heterogeneous deployment as central mechanisms for reducing
cellular energy use \cite{hasan2011green}.  EARTH-style evaluation frameworks
argue that energy claims need network-level traffic and power models, not only
per-node power accounting \cite{auer2011energy}.  Cell zooming similarly shows
that traffic-aware coverage adaptation can save energy, but also couples energy
control to user demand, radio geometry, and neighboring-cell load
\cite{niu2010cellzooming}.  UNO-UMB builds on this line of work, but focuses on
the safety of sleep decisions under shifted or mispredicted demand rather than
only on finding low-load intervals.

Digital-twin network surveys position virtual network replicas as tools for
monitoring, policy evaluation, and network management \cite{wu2021digitaltwin}.
The ns-3 LTE simulator has also been used as an open substrate for evaluating
LTE radio-resource and self-optimization behavior before deployment
\cite{baldo2011lte}.  UNO-UMB takes a deliberately lightweight version of this
idea: the twin is not a high-fidelity replica of an operator network, but a
small risk model that predicts whether a proposed sleep placement will overload
the remaining active cells.

The forecast margin in UNO-UMB is related to the broader robust optimization
tradeoff between protection against uncertainty and the cost of conservative
decisions \cite{bertsimas2004price}.  The paper's contribution is to make this
tradeoff spatially conditional for cell sleep: the controller inflates the
forecast bound only when tight utilization slack coincides with close offload
geometry, and the evaluation counts safety only on rows that are feasible in
the all-on reference.

\section{Conclusion}

UNO-UMB shows that cell sleep safety can benefit from separating reactive and
forecasted uncertainty.  Latent-demand wakeup protects feasible unannounced
center bursts, while selective overlap-gated forecast margins protect hard
right-edge transitions without imposing the full global conservative energy
cost.  The current ns-3 prototype and experiment notes are ready for a first
manuscript review and a broader UE-count and load sweep.
