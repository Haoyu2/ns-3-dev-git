\begin{abstract}
Cell sleep can reduce radio access network energy use, but demand shifts can
turn apparently safe sleep decisions into quality-of-service violations.
This paper presents UNO-UMB, an ns-3 prototype for QoS-safe RAN cell sleep
under shifted and mispredicted traffic.  UNO-UMB combines a lightweight
digital-twin risk gate, a latent-demand wakeup rule for unannounced shifts,
forecast-risk margins, and a load-aware actionability gate that suppresses
small underpredicted forecasts.  In a five-seed, three-run, three-spacing LTE
evaluation, adaptive latent-demand wakeup is safe on all 44/44 feasible
center-burst rows where static twin control is safe on 40/44.  On a
right-edge transition surface with a 25\% underpredicted forecast, a selective
forecast margin reaches 39/40 safety with 13.754\% mean safe energy saving,
matching a stronger global margin that saves only 9.927\%.  On a
three-spacing UE-count/load grid, the actionability-gated adaptive controller
is safe on all 115/115 feasible rows with 19.510\% mean safe energy saving.
These results show that separating reactive, forecasted, and actionable
uncertainty can preserve energy savings while reducing transition risk.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Base stations account for a large share of cellular network energy demand, and
radio access networks are typically dimensioned for peaks even though traffic
varies sharply over time \cite{auer2011energy,hasan2011green}.  Cell sleep is
a direct energy-saving mechanism: turn off lightly loaded cells and carry their
users on neighboring cells.  The difficulty is safety.  A sleep decision changes
both load and radio geometry, so a cell that appears idle before a traffic shift
can become necessary after the shift.

UNO-UMB studies this safety problem in ns-3.  The paper asks whether a
lightweight controller can recover cell-sleep energy savings while avoiding
controller-induced failures on workloads that remain feasible when all cells
stay active.  This framing matters because not every offered-load row is
controllable: some high-load rows violate the QoS target even without cell
sleep.  UNO-UMB therefore reports safety only against matched all-on feasible
references.

The contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
\item A digital-twin cell-sleep controller that evaluates candidate sleep
placements with per-UE offered load, active-cell utilization, and offload
geometry.
\item A latent-demand wakeup mechanism that reacts to unannounced load shifts
by reactivating sleeping preferred cells when doing so would relieve active
cell stress.
\item Two forecast controls for anticipated shifts: selective forecast-risk
inflation for tight close-offload decisions, and a forecast actionability gate
that reserves early forecast updates for larger shifted-load events.
\item A feasible-row evaluation method that separates controller-induced
violations from offered-load overload by comparing each controller row against
the matching all-on reference.
\end{itemize}

\section{Controller}

UNO-UMB runs as a periodic controller with event-triggered reevaluation when
demand changes.  Let $C$ be the controller-side cell capacity and
$\hat L_j(a)$ be the predicted load on active cell $j$ after candidate sleep
action $a$.  The twin risk estimate is
\begin{equation}
  \hat \rho_{\max}(a)=\max_j \frac{\hat L_j(a)}{C}.
\end{equation}
The controller accepts a sleep action only when the utilization estimate plus
its uncertainty guard is within the configured limit:
\begin{equation}
  \hat \rho_{\max}(a) + u(a) \leq \rho_{\max}^{\mathrm{limit}}.
\end{equation}
The baseline \emph{twin} policy uses this rule to avoid sleeping cells that
would overload the remaining active set.

\subsection{Latent-Demand Wakeup}

The adaptive controller adds a reactive wakeup rule.  When a sleeping cell is
the preferred cell for enough latent offered demand, UNO-UMB estimates the peak
active-cell utilization before and after restoring that cell.  It wakes the
cell when
\begin{equation}
  L_{\mathrm{latent}} \geq L_{\min}
  \quad\text{and}\quad
  \rho_{\max}^{\mathrm{active}}-\rho_{\max}^{\mathrm{restored}}\geq \Delta_\rho .
\end{equation}
This targets unannounced shifts where a placement that was safe before the
shift becomes unsafe before the next steady-state decision.

\subsection{Forecasted Shifts}

For anticipated shifts, a forecast may specify lead time, a burst magnitude,
and a magnitude error.  If $S$ is the set of shifted UEs, $r$ is the per-UE
base rate, and $b_f$ is the forecast burst multiplier, UNO-UMB computes the
forecasted shifted extra load
\begin{equation}
  L_f = |S|r\max(b_f-1,0).
\end{equation}
The forecast is actionable only when the lead time is long enough and
$L_f \geq \tau_f$, where $\tau_f$ is the actionability threshold.  If the
forecast is not actionable, the controller suppresses both the early forecast
lead and the underpredicted forecasted rate update.  If it is actionable, the
controller applies the forecasted burst multiplier
\begin{equation}
  b_c = 1 + \max((b_f-1)(1+\epsilon),0),
\end{equation}
where $\epsilon$ is the relative magnitude error.

The forecast uncertainty margin adds a utilization guard during the forecast
window:
\begin{equation}
  u_f=\frac{|S|r\max(b_f-1,0)q}{C},
\end{equation}
where $q$ is the forecast uncertainty bound.  The selective-overlap variant
uses a base $q$ for ordinary forecasted decisions and raises $q$ only when a
candidate decision has small utilization slack and close offload geometry.

A row is \emph{all-on feasible} when the matching all-on run does not violate
the QoS target.  A controller row is \emph{safe} when the all-on row is feasible
and the controller row has no QoS violation and no unsafe sleep action.  Safe
energy saving is reported only over safe controller rows:
\begin{equation}
  E_{\mathrm{save}} = 100\left(1-\frac{E_{\mathrm{controller}}}{E_{\mathrm{all-on}}}\right).
\end{equation}
This avoids giving credit for saving energy by dropping traffic in overloaded
or controller-induced-failure cases.

\section{Discussion}

The strongest result is not that a single conservative margin solves all
right-edge transitions.  Instead, the results show that different uncertainty
sources need different controls.  Unannounced shifts require reactive wakeup.
Forecasted hard transitions require forecast uncertainty margins.  Small
underpredicted forecast events can be safer when treated as reactive events,
which is exactly what the actionability gate enforces.

The forecast-error stress check also shows non-monotonic behavior near the QoS
threshold.  More accurate or more conservative forecast magnitudes do not
automatically dominate the calibrated setting because early wakeups, handovers,
and scheduler transients can interact with rows that are barely feasible in the
all-on reference.  This motivates treating forecast magnitude, uncertainty
margin, and actionability threshold as a coupled design space.

The feasible-row denominator is important for interpretation.  Some high-load
rows are infeasible even when all cells remain active; those rows are excluded
from controller safety rates.  This makes the claims narrower but cleaner: the
paper measures whether cell sleep creates avoidable failures on workloads that
the network can otherwise serve.

The current prototype remains a controlled LTE study.  It uses a three-cell
line topology, analytical power constants, and ideal RRC signaling for the
paper-facing sweeps.  Stronger baselines should include calibrated static
margins, policies with forecast lead but no actionability gate, and stress
tests over larger forecast errors and mobility.

\section{Related Work}

RAN energy-saving work has long identified base-station sleep, adaptive
coverage, and heterogeneous deployment as central mechanisms for reducing
cellular energy use \cite{hasan2011green}.  EARTH-style evaluation frameworks
argue that energy claims need network-level traffic and power models, not only
per-node power accounting \cite{auer2011energy}.  Cell zooming similarly shows
that traffic-aware coverage adaptation can save energy, but also couples energy
control to user demand, radio geometry, and neighboring-cell load
\cite{niu2010cellzooming}.  UNO-UMB builds on this line of work, but focuses on
the safety of sleep decisions under shifted or mispredicted demand rather than
only on finding low-load intervals.

Digital-twin network surveys position virtual network replicas as tools for
monitoring, policy evaluation, and network management \cite{wu2021digitaltwin}.
The ns-3 LTE simulator has also been used as an open substrate for evaluating
LTE radio-resource and self-optimization behavior before deployment
\cite{baldo2011lte}.  UNO-UMB takes a deliberately lightweight version of this
idea: the twin is not a high-fidelity replica of an operator network, but a
small risk model that predicts whether a proposed sleep placement will overload
the remaining active cells.

The forecast margin in UNO-UMB is related to the broader robust optimization
tradeoff between protection against uncertainty and the cost of conservative
decisions \cite{bertsimas2004price}.  The paper's contribution is to make this
tradeoff conditional for cell sleep: uncertainty is inflated only for specific
forecasted, geometric, or actionability regimes, and safety is counted only on
rows that are feasible in the all-on reference.

\section{Conclusion}

UNO-UMB demonstrates conditional uncertainty control for QoS-safe RAN cell
sleep.  Latent-demand wakeup protects feasible unannounced center bursts.
Selective forecast margins protect hard right-edge transitions without the full
energy cost of a global conservative margin.  The actionability gate combines
these ideas across a broader UE-count, load, and spacing grid, reaching 115/115
safe feasible right-edge rows with 19.510\% mean safe energy saving.  These
results provide a concrete ns-3 foundation for evaluating conditional
uncertainty control under forecast error, load variation, and mobility.
